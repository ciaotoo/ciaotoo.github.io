Problem

As our design teams grow and hold key roles in cross-platform products, we became increasingly aware that our disconnected structure was an issue. Given that we were building experiences off an existing UI kit, problems arose as we were frequently unable to pinpoint why or when to use a component, or why we may have had numerous unnecessary instances of it. Although it was created as a method to streamline design efforts, the kit resulted in a low return on investment with longer times to market and frustrations while also leaving developers disjointed from many conversations.

Although initially met with skepticism, I was able to advocate the creation of a design system consisting of documented guidelines, working code, extensive resources, and human interface guidelines to build consistent digital experiences faster. Rather than maximizing time spent in design and development, which accumulates technical and design debt, we needed documented structure to improve our practices, leading to more time spent on advancing external and internal experiences.

Working with the ambiguity and unstructured nature of our UI kit (right) led to more problems than benefits, with components being hosted and undocumented in both Figma and AdobeXD.

Design System Values

With an evolving product strategy and visual language, we determined our methods had to change too. Avoiding the pitfalls of creating a one-size-fits-all solution, we dove head first into defining a bespoke solution for our company’s needs.

Given the temporary nature of my position, I had to ensure this system was outcome-driven, emphasizing a long-term solution that is stable, dependable, and can be improved on after I’m gone. Recognizing that Liz is our digital extension of the Safe brand and acts as the foundation for every digital experience at Safe, we defined a set of values to ensure we deliver flexibility, consistency, and quality to all our end users.

An Abundance of Debt

Having logged every component and layout used across our site with my lead, we recreated each element in a Figma board as part of our inventory and audit process. Any skepticism towards the need of a design system was wiped when we visualized the technical debt across our product. With literally dozens of card, layout, and button styles, misued type weights and sizes, and poorly scaled elements everywhere, there was less structure than a late 1800s mining town.

After multiple rounds of review, we compiled our fundamental components onto a final page to begin documentation for our pattern library. Working closely with our developers, we decided to ensure each component page should at least have a name, description of its parts and use in context, code chunks, and a changelog.

A Varied Audience

Given our scattered design team structure and how each development team handles their backend, I facilitated numerous discussions to ensure my outcome was both immediately usable, and flexible enough for future iteration through the development pipeline.

What we needed was structure that would scale with the company's growth, while being accessible, sustainable, and collaborative—something that would scale across multiple platforms and use cases. Coupled with thorough documentation, I designed and helped build components that follow consistent naming conventions, layer structures, styling, and more.

Designing at Scale

Given the sudden transition from a long-standing UI kit to a structured design system, a major concern gathered from messaging team members was the speed of how we’d approach transforming our platforms. We recognized that although 100% change is not attainable, many 1% changes through marginal gains is much more feasible and sustainable.

Our success is ultimately measured from our outcomes and not our outputs. We’re building trust with people, and we want people around for a long time. Results

Albeit what I was looking forward to the most, designing each component had its own share of difficulties. From ensuring every layer met strict naming standards, to recognizing to what the extent I can push our visual style, it has been incredibly rewarding to use, and see others make use of our new components. Surprisingly, the work done with our design system had prompted a developer to look into a new css framework, Tailwind, which will inevitably be used to structure our pattern library on the front end.

An Ongoing Process

This project allowed me to practice systems thinking, regularly managing collaboration with developers, and human-centered design thinking to consider all the required features of what has become a widely used resource. I explored opportunities to experiment with unique patterns and interactions while exploring undefined use-cases that were not initially captured in my project’s brief.

I’m proud to have delivered a comprehensive product that is living, and ever-changing, with the ability to be further adapted and localized for use across specific products in Safe’s lineup. This experience helped me become proactive in leading conversations, determining what to pull from large amounts of feedback, updating stakeholders, and selling an idea based on gathered data. It is incredibly rewarding to know that my internship project is actively used and modified by the growing design team at Safe.
